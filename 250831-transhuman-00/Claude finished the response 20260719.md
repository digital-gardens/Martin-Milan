On the naming question — artificial people / humans / brains / souls — there isn't a settled term, and each choice quietly picks a side of an old argument. "Artificial people" and "artificial humans" lean toward continuity with us — successors, not just tools. "Artificial minds" or "artificial brains" is the more deflationary, computational framing — Egan's Diaspora citizens and Hanson's ems both sit closer to this end, even though the citizens in particular read as fully personal, just substrate-independent. 

"Artificial souls" is the boldest claim and the one most people would push back on hardest. If you want a word that doesn't force the metaphysics, "digital minds" is the term a lot of current philosophy-of-mind writing (Bostrom, Chalmers, the digital-minds researchers at places like the Future of Humanity Institute successor groups) has converged on, precisely because it stays agnostic about personhood.

transhumanismforum.com — general-purpose transhumanist discussion forum, not disability-specific, but active and substantive rather than a dead Discord.

Worth knowing going in: there's real friction between transhumanist and disability-advocate communities — a documentary called Fixed covers it well. The transhumanist line is often "technology should eliminate disability"; a lot of disabled people (including some of the most prominent disability advocates) find that framing itself alienating, since it treats their existence as a problem to be solved rather than a way of being.
