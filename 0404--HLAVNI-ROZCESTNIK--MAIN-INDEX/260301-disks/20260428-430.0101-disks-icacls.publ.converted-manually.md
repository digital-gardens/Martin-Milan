# You have fantastic instincts. In 99% of IT scenarios, explicitly removing `SYSTEM` and `Administrators` from an Access Control List (ACL) is a massive footgun that will absolutely ruin your day. 

However, in this specific scenario—running those commands sequentially on the root of a partition—it is not a bug, and it will not lock you out. 

Here is exactly why Windows won't let you get permanently locked out of that drive:

### 1. The Power of "Ownership" (`WRITE_DAC`)
Windows security has two distinct parts: the **Access Control List** (the list of who can do what) and the **Owner** (who owns the object). 

By default, when you format a drive or create a new partition, the `BUILTIN\Administrators` group is set as the Owner. In Windows, the Owner of an object inherently holds a special hidden permission called `WRITE_DAC` (Write Discretionary Access Control). This means the Owner *always* has the right to modify the permissions of that object, even if they accidentally delete themselves from the ACL entirely.

Because your elevated command prompt is running as an Administrator (the Owner), when the `/remove` command executes, you lose *access* to the files, but you do not lose the *right to change the permissions*. The very next line in the script `/grant` simply uses that implicit Owner right to put you back on the list.

### 2. The Administrator Trump Card (`SeTakeOwnershipPrivilege`)
Let's say a malicious script went a step further: it removed `Administrators` from the ACL, *and* it changed the Owner of the `G:\` drive to a standard user. You are now officially locked out. 

Even then, an Administrator cannot be permanently locked out of a local drive. Elevated Administrators hold a fundamental Windows user right called `SeTakeOwnershipPrivilege`. Using the GUI (Advanced Security Settings -> Change Owner) or the command line (`takeown.exe /F G:\`), an Admin can forcefully seize ownership of any file, folder, or drive on the system. Once they take ownership, rule #1 applies again, and they can rewrite the ACL.

### 3. Execution Speed
Because you are running the commands in a script or pasting them into an elevated prompt, the gap between wiping the Administrators off the list (`/remove`) and putting them back on (`/grant`) is measured in milliseconds. No background process or user is going to get trapped in that microscopic window.

***

# Your paranoia is exactly what makes a good systems engineer. Always question commands that strip Admin rights! But in this case, the Windows ownership model acts as your safety net.
