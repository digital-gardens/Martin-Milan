Therapeutic yoga workshop, Jakarta, 2016

**Yoga as therapy** is the use of [yoga as exercise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_as_exercise "Yoga as exercise"), consisting mainly of postures called [asanas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asana "Asana"), as a gentle form of [exercise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exercise "Exercise") and relaxation applied specifically with the intention of improving health. This form of [yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga "Yoga") is widely practised in classes, and may involve [meditation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditation "Meditation"), imagery, [breath work (pranayama)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pranayama "Pranayama") and calming [music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music "Music") as well as postural yoga.[^1]

At least three types of health claims have been made for yoga: [magical claims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddhi "Siddhi") for medieval [haṭha yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ha%E1%B9%ADha_yoga "Haṭha yoga"), including the power of healing; unsupported claims of benefits to [organ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_\(anatomy\) "Organ (anatomy)") systems from the practice of asanas; and more or less well supported claims of specific medical and psychological benefits from studies of differing sizes using a wide variety of methodologies.

[Systematic reviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematic_review "Systematic review") have found some evidence of beneficial effects of yoga on [low back pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_back_pain "Low back pain") [^2] and [depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depression_\(mood\) "Depression (mood)"),[^3] but despite much investigation, little or no evidence of benefit for specific medical conditions.[^3] [^4] The study of [trauma-sensitive yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trauma-sensitive_yoga "Trauma-sensitive yoga") has been hampered by weak methodology.[^5]

## Context

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Yoga_dimensions.svg/500px-Yoga_dimensions.svg.png)

Different schools teach yoga with emphasis on aerobic exercise (such as Bikram Yoga ), precision in the asanas (like Iyengar Yoga ), or spirituality (like Sivananda Yoga ). 6 Unbranded "hatha yoga" (not to be confused with medieval haṭha yoga ) may teach any combination of these.

[Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_as_exercise "Yoga as exercise") classes used as therapy usually consist of [asanas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asana "Asana") (postures used for stretching), [pranayama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pranayama "Pranayama") (breathing exercises), and relaxation in [savasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savasana "Savasana") (lying down).[^7] The physical asanas of modern yoga are related to medieval [haṭha yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ha%E1%B9%ADha_yoga "Haṭha yoga") tradition, but they were not widely practiced in India before the early 20th century.[^8]

The number of [schools and styles of yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_yoga_schools "List of yoga schools") in the Western world has grown rapidly from the late 20th century. By 2012, there were at least 19 widespread styles from [Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashtanga_Vinyasa_Yoga "Ashtanga Vinyasa Yoga") to [Viniyoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viniyoga "Viniyoga"). These emphasise different aspects including [aerobic exercise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_exercise "Aerobic exercise"), precision in the asanas, and spirituality in the haṭha yoga tradition.[^6] [^9] These aspects can be illustrated by schools with distinctive styles. [Bikram Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bikram_Yoga "Bikram Yoga") has an aerobic exercise style with rooms heated to 105 °F (41 °C) and a fixed sequence of 2 breathing exercises and 26 asanas performed in every session. [Iyengar Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iyengar_Yoga "Iyengar Yoga") emphasises correct alignment in the postures, working slowly, if necessary with props, and ending with relaxation. [Sivananda Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sivananda_Yoga "Sivananda Yoga") focuses more on spiritual practice, with 12 basic poses, chanting in [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit"), pranayama breathing exercises, [meditation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditation "Meditation"), and relaxation in each class, and importance is placed on a [vegetarian diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetarianism "Vegetarianism").[^6] [^9]

## Types of claims

At least three different types of claims of therapeutic benefit have been made for yoga from medieval times onwards, not counting the more general claims of good health made throughout this period: magical powers, biomedical claims for marketing purposes, and specific medical claims. Neither of the first two are supported by reliable evidence. The medical claims are supported by evidence of varying quality, from [case studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Case_study "Case study") to [controlled trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Controlled_trial "Controlled trial") and ultimately [systematic review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematic_review "Systematic review") of multiple trials.[^10] [^11]

### Magical powers

Medieval authors asserted that [Haṭha yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ha%E1%B9%ADha_yoga "Haṭha yoga") brought physical (as well as spiritual) benefits, and provided magical powers, including of healing. The *[Hatha Yoga Pradipika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatha_Yoga_Pradipika "Hatha Yoga Pradipika")* (HYP) states that asanas in general, described as the first auxiliary of haṭha yoga, give "steadiness, good health, and lightness of limb." (HYP 1.17) [^10] Specific asanas, it claims, bring additional benefits; for example, [Matsyendrasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matsyendrasana "Matsyendrasana") awakens [Kundalini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kundalini "Kundalini") and helps to prevent [semen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semen "Semen") from being shed involuntarily; (HYP 1.27) [Paschimottanasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paschimottanasana "Paschimottanasana") "stokes up the digestive fire, slims the belly and gives good health"; (HYP 1.29) [Shavasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shavasana "Shavasana") "takes away fatigue and relaxes the mind"; (HYP 1.32) while [Padmasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lotus_position "Lotus position") "destroys all diseases" (HYP 1.47).[^12] These claims lie within a tradition across all forms of yoga that practitioners can gain [supernatural powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddhi "Siddhi").[^13] [Hemachandra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemachandra "Hemachandra") 's *Yogashastra* (1.8–9) lists the magical powers, which include healing and the destruction of poisons.[^14]

### Biomedical claims for marketing purposes

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Uttitha_Trikonasana.jpg/250px-Uttitha_Trikonasana.jpg)

Styles of yoga differ in their approach to the asanas. Iyengar Yoga emphasises correctness, seen here as a practitioner uses a yoga brick to attain correct alignment in Utthitha Trikonasana. 9

Twentieth century advocates of some schools of yoga, such as [B. K. S. Iyengar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B._K._S._Iyengar "B. K. S. Iyengar"), have for various reasons made claims for the effects of yoga on specific organs, without citing any evidence. The yoga scholar [Suzanne Newcombe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzanne_Newcombe "Suzanne Newcombe") argues that this was one of several visions of yoga as in some sense therapeutic, ranging from medical to a more popular offer of health and [well-being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-being "Well-being").[^15] The yoga scholar [Andrea Jain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrea_Jain "Andrea Jain") describes these claims of Iyengar's in terms of "elaborating and fortifying his yoga brand" [^16] and "mass-marketing",[^16] calling Iyengar's 1966 book *[Light on Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_on_Yoga "Light on Yoga")* "arguably the most significant event in the process of elaborating the brand." [^16] The yoga teacher Bernie Gourley notes that the book neither describes [contraindications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication "Contraindication") systematically, nor provides evidence for the claimed benefits.[^17] Jain suggests that "Its biomedical dialect was attractive to many." [^16] For example, in the book, Iyengar claims that the asanas of the [Eka Pada Sirsasana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eka_Pada_Sirsasana "Eka Pada Sirsasana") cycle [^18]

> ...tone up the muscular, nervous and circulatory systems of the entire body. The spine receives a rich supply of blood, which increases the nervous energy in the [chakras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakra "Chakra") (the various nerve plexuses situated in the spine), the [flywheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flywheel "Flywheel") in the human body machine. These poses develop the chest and make the breathing fuller and the body firmer; they stop nervous trembling of the body and prevent the diseases which cause it; they also help to eliminate toxins by supplying pure blood to every part of the body and bringing the congested blood back to the heart and lungs for purification.[^18]

The history of such claims was reviewed by [William J. Broad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_J._Broad "William J. Broad") in his 2012 book *The Science of Yoga*. Broad argues that while the health claims for yoga began as [Hindu nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_nationalist "Hindu nationalist") posturing, it turns out that there is ironically [^11] "a wealth of real benefits".[^11]

### Possible mechanisms

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Padangushtasana%2C_Heinz_Grill_1992_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Padangushtasana%2C_Heinz_Grill_1992_%28cropped%29.jpg)

One of the possible mechanisms for therapeutic effects of yoga is the mastery of difficult asanas, here Padangushtasana demonstrated by Heinz Grill, which could prompt practitioners to reconsider limiting assumptions in their lives. 19

The psychiatrist [Bessel van der Kolk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_van_der_Kolk "Bessel van der Kolk") in his 2014 book *[The Body Keeps the Score](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Body_Keeps_the_Score "The Body Keeps the Score")*, and the scholar of religion [Paul Bramadat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Bramadat "Paul Bramadat") (who cites van der Kolk and adds his own reasoning) in his 2025 book *Yogalands*, have proposed multiple mechanisms by which yoga could provide therapeutic benefits.[^19] In their opinions, these hypotheses remained under-researched at the times they were writing.[^19]

## Types of activity

### Remedial yoga

The [International Association of Yoga Therapists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Association_of_Yoga_Therapists "International Association of Yoga Therapists") offers a definition of yoga therapy that can encompass a wide range of activities and practices, calling it "the process of empowering individuals to progress toward improved health and well-being through the application of the teachings and practices of Yoga".[^20]

The history of remedial yoga goes back to the pioneers of [yoga as exercise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_as_exercise "Yoga as exercise"), [Krishnamacharya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krishnamacharya "Krishnamacharya") and Iyengar. Iyengar was sickly as a child, and yoga with his brother-in-law Krishnamacharya improved his health; it had also helped his daughter [Geeta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geeta_Iyengar "Geeta Iyengar"), so his response to his students' health issues, in Newcombe's view, "was an intense and personal one." [^21] In effect Iyengar was treating "remedial yoga" as analogous to [Henrik Ling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henrik_Ling "Henrik Ling") 's medical gymnastics.[^21] As early as 1940, Iyengar was using yoga as a therapy for common conditions such as sinus problems, backache, and fatigue.[^22] Iyengar was willing to push people through pain "to \[show\] them new possibilities." [^23] In the 1960s, he trained a few people such as Diana Clifton and [Silva Mehta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silva_Mehta "Silva Mehta") to deliver this remedial yoga; particular asanas were used for different conditions, and non-remedial Iyengar Yoga teachers were taught to inform students that ordinary classes were not suitable for "serious health issues".[^24] Mehta taught a remedial yoga class in the Iyengar Yoga Institute in [Maida Vale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maida_Vale "Maida Vale") from its opening in 1984.[^25] She contributed "Remedial Programs" for conditions such as [arthritis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthritis "Arthritis"), [backache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backache "Backache"), [knee cartilage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knee_cartilage "Knee cartilage") problems, [pregnancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pregnancy "Pregnancy"), [sciatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sciatica "Sciatica"), [scoliosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scoliosis "Scoliosis") and [varicose veins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varicose_veins "Varicose veins") in the Mehtas' 1990 book *[Yoga the Iyengar Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_the_Iyengar_Way "Yoga the Iyengar Way")*.[^26] However, Iyengar was deferential to Western medicine and its assessments, so in Newcombe's view Iyengar Yoga is "positioned as complementary to standard medical treatment rather than as an alternative".[^27]

Newcombe argues that in Britain, yoga "largely avoided overt conflict with the medical profession by simultaneously [professionalising with educational qualifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_teacher_training "Yoga teacher training") and deferring to medical expertise." [^28] After [Richard Hittleman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Hittleman "Richard Hittleman") 's *[Yoga for Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_for_Health "Yoga for Health")* series on [ITV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITV_\(TV_network\) "ITV (TV network)") from 1971 to 1974,[^29] the series producer Howard Kent founded a [charity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Registered_charity_in_England "Registered charity in England"), the Yoga for Health Foundation, for "Research into the therapeutic benefits to be obtained by the practice of yoga";[^30] residential courses began in 1978 at [Ickwell Bury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ickwell_Bury "Ickwell Bury") in Bedfordshire.[^31] The Foundation stated that yoga was not a therapy or cure but had "therapeutic benefits", whether physical, mental, or emotional, and it worked especially with "the physically handicapped".[^32] Newcombe notes that a third organisation, the Yoga Biomedical Trust, was founded in [Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge "Cambridge") in 1983 by a biologist, Robin Monro, to research complementary therapies. He found it difficult to obtain [research funding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_funding "Research funding"), and in the 1990s moved to London, focusing on training yoga teachers in yoga as therapy and providing yoga as individualised therapy, using pranayama, relaxation and asanas.[^33]

### Sports medicine

From the point of view of [sports medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sports_medicine "Sports medicine"), asanas function as active stretches, helping to protect [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle "Muscle") from [injury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle_strain "Muscle strain"); these need to be performed equally on both sides, the stronger side first if used for [physical rehabilitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_rehabilitation "Physical rehabilitation").[^34]

## Research

### Methodology

Much of the research on the therapeutic use of yoga has been in the form of preliminary studies or [clinical trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_trial "Clinical trial") of low methodological quality, including small sample sizes, inadequate [control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control "Scientific control") and blinding, lack of [randomization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_experiment "Randomized experiment"), and high risk of [bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias "Bias").[^35] [^4] Further research is needed to quantify the benefits and to clarify the mechanisms involved.[^36]

For example, a 2010 literature review on the use of yoga for [depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depression_\(mood\) "Depression (mood)") stated, "although the results from these trials are encouraging, they should be viewed as very preliminary because the trials, as a group, suffered from substantial methodological limitations." [^4] A 2015 [systematic review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematic_review "Systematic review") on the effect of yoga on mood and the brain recommended that future clinical trials should apply more methodological rigour.[^3]

### Mechanisms

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/YogaClass.jpg/250px-YogaClass.jpg)

A yoga class relaxing in Supta Baddha Konasana. There is evidence that yoga relieves stress by multiple mechanisms. 37

The practice of asanas has been claimed to improve flexibility, strength, and balance; to alleviate stress and anxiety, and to reduce the symptoms of [lower back pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_back_pain "Lower back pain"), without necessarily demonstrating the precise mechanisms involved.[^38] A review of five studies noted that three psychological mechanisms ([positive affect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_affect "Positive affect"), [mindfulness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mindfulness "Mindfulness"), [self-compassion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-compassion "Self-compassion")) and four biological mechanisms (posterior [hypothalamus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus"), [interleukin-6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interleukin-6 "Interleukin-6"), [C-reactive protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-reactive_protein "C-reactive protein") and [cortisol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cortisol "Cortisol")) that might act on stress had been examined empirically, whereas many other potential mechanisms remain to be studied; four of the mechanisms (positive affect, self-compassion, inhibition of the posterior hypothalamus and salivary cortisol) were found to mediate yoga's effect on [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_\(biology\) "Stress (biology)").[^37]

### Low back pain

Back pain is one reason people take up yoga, and since at least the 1960s some practitioners have claimed that it relieved their symptoms.[^39] Yoga results in clinically unimportant improvements in pain and back-specific function; improvements in back-specific function are similar to those obtained from other forms of therapeutic exercise, such as physical therapy.[^2]

### Mental disorders

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Ptsd_figure.png/250px-Ptsd_figure.png)

Trauma-sensitive yoga has been developed in the US in the hope of benefiting individuals suffering from psychological trauma.

Yoga can form a useful component of treatment for [PTSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PTSD "PTSD"), though its effect is no different from other forms of physical exercise.[^40]

### Cardiovascular health

A 2012 survey of yoga in Australia notes that there is "good evidence" [^41] that yoga and its associated [healthy lifestyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthy_lifestyle "Healthy lifestyle") —often [vegetarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetarian "Vegetarian"), usually [non-smoking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_effects_of_tobacco "Health effects of tobacco"), preferring [organic food](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_food "Organic food"), [drinking less or no alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_consumption_and_health "Alcohol consumption and health") –are beneficial for [cardiovascular health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiovascular_health "Cardiovascular health"), but that there was "little apparent uptake of yoga to address \[existing\] cardiovascular conditions and risk factors".[^36] Yoga was cited by respondents as a cause of these lifestyle changes. The survey notes that the relative importance of the various factors had not been assessed.[^36]

### Other conditions

There is little reliable evidence that yoga is beneficial for specific medical conditions, and an increasing amount of evidence that it is not.[^42] [^43] [^44] [^45] [^46] For instance it offers no benefit in [ADHD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder "Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder").[^35]

## Safety

Although relatively safe, yoga is not a risk-free form of exercise. Sensible precautions can usefully be taken, such as avoiding advanced moves by beginners, not combining practice with psychoactive drug use, and avoiding competitiveness.[^52]
